## 1. Overview

Artificial intelligence is generating a new category of evidence — transcripts produced by speech-to-text models, images synthesised or enhanced by generative AI, predictive analytical reports, AI-reconstructed CCTV footage, and outputs of large language models used in forensic analysis. Indian courts are increasingly confronted with such material, yet the evidentiary framework governing its admissibility remains rooted in statutes designed for paper documents and human testimony.

This case study examines the admissibility of AI-generated evidence under the **Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 (BSA)** — which replaced the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 with effect from 1 July 2024 — alongside the residual role of the **Information Technology Act, 2000** and the constitutional guarantees of a fair trial under **Article 21**.

---

## 2. The AI Fact-Pattern

> **Hypothetical A (Criminal):** The prosecution in a murder trial seeks to admit an AI-enhanced reconstruction of CCTV footage — produced by a neural network upscaling tool — as evidence of the accused's presence at the scene. The original footage is grainy and inconclusive; the AI reconstruction shows a face the prosecution claims matches the accused. The defence challenges the reconstruction as fabricated or unreliable.

> **Hypothetical B (Civil):** A party in a commercial dispute produces a contract analysis report generated by a large language model, identifying clauses it claims were breached. The opposing party objects that the report is hearsay, not the product of a human expert, and cannot be cross-examined.

Both fact-patterns are live issues in Indian litigation and expose distinct gaps in the evidentiary framework.

## 3. The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 — Key Provisions

### 3.1 Section 57 — Admissibility of Electronic Records

The BSA consolidates and updates the evidentiary treatment of electronic records. Section 57 BSA (corresponding to Section 65B of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872) governs the admissibility of electronic records and requires a **certificate** from a responsible official attesting that:

- The electronic record was produced by a computer during ordinary use
- The computer was operating properly during the relevant period
- The information was regularly fed into the computer in the ordinary course of activities

**Application to AI-Generated Evidence:**  
An AI reconstruction of CCTV footage is not a straightforward "electronic record" in the Section 57 sense — it is a *derivative output* generated by a neural network model applied to an original record. The certificate mechanism was designed for records *stored* or *transmitted* electronically, not for records *created* by an AI inference process. The original footage may satisfy Section 57; the AI reconstruction almost certainly does not without additional foundation.

**Gap Identified:**  
Section 57 BSA does not contemplate AI-generated derivative outputs. The certificate mechanism is inadequate to authenticate a neural network reconstruction — it cannot attest to the reliability of the model's training data, architecture, or inference methodology.

---

### 3.2 Section 39 — Expert Opinion

Section 39 BSA (corresponding to Section 45 of the Indian Evidence Act) permits courts to receive opinion evidence from experts in science, art, foreign law, or handwriting when the court has to form an opinion on a point requiring specialised knowledge.

**Application:**  
An AI system is not a "person" and cannot give expert opinion evidence. However, a human expert — a forensic AI specialist or computer scientist — may give opinion evidence *about* the AI output: explaining the model's methodology, its known error rates, its validation history, and the reliability of the specific output. This is analogous to a forensic expert explaining DNA test results produced by an automated sequencing machine.

**Critical distinction:**  
The AI output itself is not admissible as expert opinion. The human expert's opinion *about* the AI output may be admissible under Section 39 BSA, provided the expert has sufficient knowledge of the specific model and its application.

**Gap Identified:**  
There are no Indian standards for qualifying an "AI forensic expert." Courts currently have no framework to assess whether a witness claiming expertise in neural network reconstruction is genuinely qualified, or what methodology should be accepted as reliable.

---

### 3.3 Section 56 — Relevance and Reliability

Section 56 BSA preserves the foundational requirement that evidence must be **relevant** to a fact in issue. Relevance alone, however, does not guarantee admissibility — evidence may be relevant but excluded if its prejudicial effect outweighs its probative value, or if its reliability is fundamentally compromised.

**Application:**  
An AI reconstruction that fills in pixel data not present in the original footage is not merely *enhancing* existing evidence — it is *creating* new visual information. If the court cannot verify whether the reconstruction accurately reflects reality or reflects the model's training biases, the evidence lacks the reliability threshold for admissibility. Admitting it may create a powerful but misleading impression on the fact-finder.

---

### 3.4 Section 2(1)(c) — Definition of "Document"

The BSA defines "document" broadly to include any matter expressed or described upon any substance by means of letters, figures, or marks. Electronic records are included by virtue of Section 57.

**Application:**  
An LLM-generated contract analysis report is a "document" for BSA purposes. However, its *contents* — being the opinion of a machine rather than a human — raise hearsay and reliability concerns that the document definition does not resolve.

## 4. The Section 65B Certificate Problem — Post-*Arjun Panditrao*

The Supreme Court in **Arjun Panditrao Khotkar v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal** (2020) 7 SCC 1 held that the Section 65B certificate (now Section 57 BSA certificate) is a **mandatory pre-condition** for admissibility of electronic records — not a mere technicality. The certificate must be produced at the time of tendering the evidence.

**Application to AI Evidence:**  
For AI-generated evidence, the *Arjun Panditrao* requirement creates a structural problem:

- Who is the "responsible official" for an AI reconstruction — the operator of the tool, the developer of the model, or the forensic laboratory?
- Can a certificate attest to the proper functioning of a neural network in the same way it attests to the proper functioning of a server or hard drive?
- Does the certificate requirement extend to the *model* used for reconstruction, or only to the hardware on which it ran?

None of these questions has been resolved by Indian courts.

## 5. Hearsay and the LLM Problem

### 5.1 The Hearsay Concern

Indian evidence law, like common law systems generally, is cautious about out-of-court statements tendered for the truth of their contents. An LLM-generated report asserting that "Clause 7 of the contract was breached" is not a statement by a person — it is an output generated by a statistical model trained on text. It cannot be:

- Cross-examined
- Sworn under oath
- Held accountable for false statements

**Application:**  
Courts admitting LLM outputs as proof of the facts they assert — without human expert attestation — risk admitting unreliable, hallucinated, or biased machine outputs as evidence of legal facts. The BSA contains no explicit rule excluding or regulating such outputs.

### 5.2 The Hallucination Risk

LLMs are known to "hallucinate" — generating plausible but factually incorrect outputs with high apparent confidence. An LLM-generated forensic report that cites non-existent case law, misquotes statutory provisions, or fabricates transactional details poses a direct risk to the integrity of judicial proceedings.

**Gap Identified:**  
The BSA contains no provision requiring disclosure of AI tool usage in producing documentary evidence, no requirement for human verification of AI outputs before tendering, and no sanction for tendering AI-generated hallucinated content as evidence.

## 6. Constitutional Dimension — Article 21 and Fair Trial

The Supreme Court has read the right to a fair trial into Article 21 (*Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar*, AIR 1979 SC 1360). A fair trial requires that the accused have a meaningful opportunity to challenge the evidence against them.

**Application:**  
Where prosecution evidence is generated by an opaque AI model — and the defence has no access to the model's architecture, training data, validation reports, or error rates — the right to effectively challenge that evidence is hollow. This is the Indian equivalent of the confrontation clause problem in US constitutional law (*Melendez-Diaz v. Massachusetts*, 557 US 305 (2009)).

**Gap Identified:**  
There is no Indian procedural mechanism for **pre-trial discovery of AI tool specifications** used to generate prosecution evidence — analogous to the disclosure of forensic laboratory protocols in conventional scientific evidence cases.

## 7. Comparative Snapshot

| Jurisdiction | AI Evidence Framework | Key Instrument |
|---|---|---|
| **India** | No explicit framework; Section 57 BSA applied by analogy | BSA 2023; *Arjun Panditrao* (2020) |
| **USA** | *Daubert* standard — reliability, peer review, error rate, general acceptance | *Daubert v. Merrell Dow* (1993); FRE Rule 702 |
| **United Kingdom** | Law Commission Report on Digital Evidence (2022) recommends reliability gateway | Law Commission Report No. 410 |
| **European Union** | AI Act 2024 — high-risk AI in justice must meet transparency and human oversight requirements | EU AI Act, Annex III, Article 14 |
| **Australia** | *Australian Evidence Act* s. 135 — discretion to exclude unreliable evidence | ALRC Report 138 on AI in the justice system |
| **Singapore** | Evidence Act amended 2022 to address AI-generated records | Evidence (Amendment) Act 2022 |

## 8. Proposed Judicial Test for AI Evidence Admissibility

Drawing on the *Daubert* framework (USA) and the Law Commission's reliability gateway (UK), and adapting to the BSA structure, the following test is proposed for Indian courts as a matter of judicial development pending legislative reform:

**A court admitting AI-generated evidence should satisfy itself that:**

1. **Provenance** — The original data inputs to the AI system are themselves admissible and authenticated
2. **Methodology** — The AI model's methodology is scientifically valid and has been subjected to peer review or independent validation
3. **Error rate** — The known or potential error rate of the model is disclosed and is acceptable for the purpose for which the evidence is tendered
4. **Human attestation** — A qualified human expert has reviewed the AI output and attests to its reliability in the specific case
5. **Disclosure** — The opposing party has been given access to sufficient information about the model to mount an effective challenge
6. **Proportionality** — The probative value of the AI evidence outweighs its prejudicial effect, particularly the risk of undue weight being given to algorithmic outputs by lay fact-finders

## 9. Identified Research Gaps

1. **No Indian *Daubert* equivalent:** Indian courts lack a structured reliability gateway for novel scientific evidence. The BSA's relevance framework is insufficient to screen unreliable AI outputs. Judicial development of a *Daubert*-style standard is the most urgent near-term need.

2. **Section 57 BSA certificate inadequacy:** The certificate mechanism was not designed for AI-generated derivative outputs. Legislative amendment or Supreme Court guidance is required to extend or adapt the certification requirement to AI forensic tools.

3. **No pre-trial AI evidence disclosure mechanism:** The Code of Criminal Procedure (now BNSS) and the Civil Procedure Code contain no mechanism for pre-trial discovery of AI tool specifications used to generate evidence — a gap that undermines the right to a fair trial.

4. **Deepfake detection as counter-evidence:** Where a party alleges that evidence has been fabricated by AI (a deepfake), the counter-evidence required to establish fabrication is itself AI-generated (forensic deepfake detection models). This creates a recursive evidentiary problem that Indian law has not addressed.

5. **LLM hallucination as professional misconduct:** Where a lawyer tenders an LLM-generated document containing hallucinated citations or fabricated facts, the professional responsibility implications under the Advocates Act, 1961 and Bar Council of India Rules are entirely unexplored.

## 10. Analytical Conclusion

The Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam, 2023 represents a significant modernisation of Indian evidence law, but it was enacted without explicit reference to AI-generated evidence. The Section 57 certificate mechanism, the expert opinion provisions, and the relevance framework collectively provide an *inadequate* foundation for the reliable admission or exclusion of AI-generated material.

Indian courts will face these questions imminently — CCTV reconstruction cases are already in the pipeline, and LLM-generated documents are appearing in civil proceedings. Without legislative intervention or Supreme Court guidance establishing a reliability gateway, there is a real risk of AI-generated evidence being admitted uncritically, or excluded arbitrarily, with neither outcome serving the interests of justice.

**Policy recommendations:**

1. Amend **Section 57 BSA** to include a specific certification and disclosure requirement for AI-generated derivative evidence, distinct from the standard electronic record certificate.
2. The **Supreme Court of India** should issue practice directions establishing a reliability gateway for AI forensic evidence, modelled on *Daubert* and adapted to the BSA framework.
3. The **Bar Council of India** should issue guidance on lawyers' professional responsibilities when using or tendering AI-generated material in proceedings.
4. The **National Forensic Sciences University** should develop accreditation standards for AI forensic tools used in criminal investigations, creating an approved list analogous to approved breathalyser devices.
